Outreach

Comunicating science to a broader audience

During one biology lecture in high school, my teacher stopped to told us how fascinating our world is. That everything works in a marvelous harmony. He then told us that every time that a human is born, our planet expels a small part of its atmosphere to keep a constant pressure. I was completely perplexed. My scientific literacy at the time was pretty lacking, but I knew enough to recognize that what he was saying was incorrect. The problem was that my teacher was not joking. He truly believed what he was saying. Unfortunately, this type of comments are not uncommon in my country, which has made me truly recognize the lack of scientific progress in Guatemala and most of Latin America.

Given the lack of educational infrastructure in Guatemala, curious students have to constantly rely on sources outside of their classrooms. A magnificent source of seemingly endless educational materials is the internet. However, most good-quality resources are not written in my native language, which is a challenge I myself had to overcome. This challenge only worsens the already huge barrier that people from developing countries must face when they desire to become scientists.

Motivated by this, I've decided to do a conscious effort to bring science to a wider audience, especially in Spanish. Currently, I spend some of my time sharing scientific data visualizations and making presentations.

Scientific Data Visualizations

Visualizations are extremely important for both scientific and communication purposes. Thus, I like to create and share graphs using data related to different topics (however, I've mostly focused on climate change lately). To be completely transparent, I also maintain a public GitHub repository where I keep all the scripts I’ve used to create the graphs. Here are some visualizations I’ve done:

Temperature anomalies in Central America

This image shows the temperature anomalies in Central America during June 2021. As we can appreciate, some areas in the region are almost 1°C warmer than compared to the baseline (1951-1980).

Sea Ice Extent

One of the most evident effects of global warming is the melting of the polar ice caps. This graph shows how the sea ice extent has been decreasing in the Northern Hemisphere over the past four decades.
